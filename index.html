Abstract

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models align vision and language with embodied control, but their object referring ability remains limited when relying solely on text prompts, especially in cluttered or out-of-distribution (OOD) scenes.

In this study, we introduce Point-VLA, a plug-and-play policy that augments language instructions with explicit visual cues (e.g., bounding boxes) to resolve referential ambiguity and enable precise, object-level grounding. To efficiently scale visually grounded datasets, we further develop an automatic data annotation pipeline requiring minimal human effort.

We evaluate Point-VLA on diverse real-world referring tasks and observe consistently stronger performance than text-only instruction VLAs, particularly in cluttered or unseen-object scenarios, with robust generalization. These results demonstrate that Point-VLA effectively resolves object referring ambiguity through pixel-level visual grounding, achieving more generalizable embodied control.

Motivation

Current VLA models rely solely on text to specify manipulation targets. However, language is inherently ambiguous for spatial referring — describing "the red cup to the left of the blue bottle, behind the plate" is verbose, error-prone, and often still insufficient in cluttered scenes. Even when a VLM can localize the correct object with 60–70% accuracy, the downstream text-only VLA achieves only ~25% manipulation success, revealing a fundamental language-action alignment gap.

Humans solve this effortlessly: when words fall short, we simply point. Point-VLA adopts the same intuition — augmenting language instructions with a bounding box drawn on the camera image to provide pixel-level spatial grounding. For example, instead of a complex sentence like "Pick up the bottle to the right of the leftmost bottles, in the middle of the desk", Point-VLA only needs "Pick up" plus a bounding box on the target. High-level intent stays in language; precise spatial reference moves to vision.

Method

Point-VLA introduces visually grounded instructions by overlaying bounding boxes on camera images to provide explicit pixel-level referents. This enables precise visual grounding alongside standard multi-view observations. The model is co-trained on both textual and visually grounded instructions, yielding a single unified policy that works in either mode.

Inference Pipeline: Users can provide visual grounding through GUI or gestures, combined with simple text commands.

We further adopt an automatic annotation strategy using Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) to propose target bounding boxes from demonstration videos, eliminating the need for manual labeling. Two data augmentation strategies — random translation and localized CutMix — are applied to improve generalization: random translation encourages the model to focus on relative position rather than absolute coordinates, while localized CutMix prevents overfitting to specific object appearances.

Training Pipeline: Automatic data annotation with MLLM, combined with data augmentation strategies (random translation and localized CutMix).

Results

Evaluation Tasks: Six real-world manipulation tasks spanning diverse challenges — irregular object picking, OOD object picking, cluttered scenario picking, egg-slot picking, plain placement, and egg-slot placement.

Performance Comparison

We evaluate Point-VLA against two baselines: Text VLA (text-only instructions) and Interleave-VLA (interleaved image-text instructions). Across all six tasks, Point-VLA achieves 92.5% average success rate, outperforming Text VLA by +60.1 points and Interleave-VLA by +52.5 points. The improvement is especially pronounced in challenging scenarios: in cluttered picking, Point-VLA reaches 94.3% vs. 43.3% for Text VLA; in egg-slot picking, it achieves 86.7% vs. only 10.0%.

VLM Localization vs VLA Execution Gap

A key finding is the significant gap between VLM localization accuracy and VLA execution success. Even when VLMs correctly localize targets 60–70% of the time, text-only VLAs convert this into only ~25% manipulation success. This gap arises because translating spatial understanding into precise motor actions through language alone loses critical information. Point-VLA bridges this gap by directly encoding spatial targets as visual cues, bypassing the lossy language bottleneck.

Text Compatibility & Data Scaling

Point-VLA maintains full compatibility with text-only instructions — co-training on both modalities does not degrade text-mode performance. Additionally, Point-VLA exhibits strong data scaling behavior: performance continues to improve as more visually grounded training data is added, suggesting the approach can benefit from larger-scale data collection.
